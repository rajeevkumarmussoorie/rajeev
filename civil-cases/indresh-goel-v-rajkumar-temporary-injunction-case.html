Indresh Goel v. Rajkumar

Original Suit No. 21 of 2025 | Appeal from Order No. 302 of 2025 Uttarakhand High Court

Abstract

This case examines the judicial principles governing temporary injunctions under Order XXXIX Rules 1 and 2 of the Code of Civil Procedure, 1908. The dispute highlights the impact of suppression of material facts and statutory restrictions on property transactions, particularly where transfer is restricted under special land laws.

Introduction

Temporary injunctions are equitable remedies intended to preserve the status quo during litigation. However, their grant depends on strict legal requirements including prima facie case, balance of convenience, and irreparable injury. This case illustrates how courts deny such relief where the plaintiff fails to act in good faith.

Facts of the Case

  • A land transaction was executed through an agreement to sell dated 17.02.2025.
  • The plaintiff paid approximately ₹50,00,000 as advance consideration.
  • The defendant allegedly attempted to transfer the property to third parties.
  • A separate unregistered agreement dated 18.02.2025 existed but was not disclosed.

Issues

  • Whether a prima facie case existed for granting injunction
  • Whether balance of convenience favored the plaintiff
  • Whether irreparable injury would occur without relief
  • Effect of suppression of material facts
  • Validity of agreement under statutory restrictions

Trial Court Findings

The injunction was denied due to lack of prima facie case, imbalance of convenience, absence of irreparable injury, and suppression of material facts.

The court found that the plaintiff failed to disclose key documents and could not establish a credible legal right requiring protection.

High Court Judgment

The High Court upheld the trial court’s decision and dismissed the appeal, emphasizing the importance of equitable conduct and statutory compliance.

The agreement was held unenforceable due to statutory restrictions on transfer of land belonging to a Scheduled Caste without prior permission.
  • Suppression of facts disentitled the plaintiff from relief
  • Equitable remedies require clean hands
  • Statutory law overrides contractual rights

Legal Principles

  • Temporary injunction is discretionary and not a matter of right
  • All three conditions must be satisfied cumulatively
  • Suppression of material facts bars equitable relief
  • Contracts violating statutory provisions are unenforceable

Conclusion

The case reinforces that equitable relief cannot be granted where the plaintiff lacks bona fide conduct or where the underlying transaction is legally prohibited. Courts will not intervene to protect rights arising from unenforceable agreements.
