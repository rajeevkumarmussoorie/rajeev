Rohit & Rahul Nikalje v. UIDAI

Writ Petition No. 10771 of 2025 | Bombay High Court | Judgment dated 06 May 2026

Abstract

This case addresses one of the most significant modern challenges within India’s digital identity framework — biometric mismatch and Aadhaar deactivation. The Bombay High Court examined whether genuine citizens can be left without an effective identity mechanism merely because of technological irregularities within the Aadhaar ecosystem.

The Court held that while biometric safeguards are necessary to preserve the integrity of the Aadhaar database, citizens cannot be forced into indefinite administrative uncertainty due to technical failures.

Introduction

Aadhaar has evolved into one of the foundational identity instruments in India, linking individuals to educational institutions, banking systems, welfare schemes, insurance facilities, and public services. Consequently, errors within the Aadhaar infrastructure can severely affect the daily lives of ordinary citizens.

The present case highlights the constitutional tension between technological security and procedural fairness. While the Unique Identification Authority of India (UIDAI) is obligated to maintain the integrity of biometric records, the judiciary emphasized that administrative systems must remain humane, transparent, and accessible.

Facts of the Case

  • The petitioners, Rohit and Rahul Nikalje, are twin brothers from Pune.
  • They were originally issued Aadhaar cards in 2012 while they were minors.
  • Upon attaining the prescribed age for mandatory biometric updation, they approached UIDAI authorities in 2022 for biometric updates.
  • Their update requests were repeatedly rejected due to “biometric mismatch”.
  • UIDAI officials initially advised them to seek cancellation and fresh enrollment, but later informed them that the cancellation process itself had been withdrawn.
  • Despite multiple visits to regional offices and repeated compliance with procedural requirements, no final resolution was provided.
  • The petitioners faced difficulties in higher education admissions, identity verification, and insurance access due to the absence of valid Aadhaar credentials.

Issues Before the Court

  • Whether UIDAI could indefinitely delay rectification of Aadhaar records affected by biometric mismatch.
  • Whether citizens can be denied effective identity credentials due to technological irregularities.
  • Whether fresh Aadhaar enrollment should be permitted in cases involving anomalous biometrics.
  • How constitutional fairness should operate within India’s biometric identity framework.

Arguments by the Petitioners

The petitioners argued that they had committed no fraud, impersonation, or suppression of facts. Since their original enrollment occurred during childhood, any biometric interchange or technical anomaly could not reasonably be attributed to them.

“The Petitioners have repeatedly approached the authorities and complied with every instruction given to them.”

It was further contended that UIDAI’s shifting administrative stand — from updation, to cancellation, to re-enrollment — had effectively deprived them of a valid identity document despite complete cooperation on their part.

UIDAI’s Stand

UIDAI defended the biometric safeguards embedded within the Aadhaar system and argued that the integrity of the Central Identities Data Repository could not be compromised merely to mechanically correct disputed biometric records.

UIDAI maintained that where biometric anomalies are detected, statutory safeguards require fresh verification and lawful re-enrollment procedures.

However, UIDAI also clarified before the Court that the petitioners were free to apply afresh with updated biometrics and valid supporting documents.

Observations of the Court

The Bombay High Court observed that the real grievance was not refusal of Aadhaar itself, but the absence of a clear and time-bound remedial mechanism. The Court strongly criticized the inconsistent administrative communication issued to the petitioners.

The Court held that the statutory framework under the Aadhaar Act, 2016 does not permit citizens to remain in “administrative limbo”.

Referring to the Supreme Court judgment in Justice K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India, the Bench reiterated that Aadhaar administration must function in a constitutionally fair, transparent, and proportionate manner.

The Court emphasized that while database integrity remains a matter of national importance, technological systems cannot become instruments of exclusion against genuine residents.

Final Directions Issued

  • The petitioners were directed to submit fresh Aadhaar enrollment applications with fresh biometric capture.
  • UIDAI authorities were ordered to accept and process the applications in accordance with law.
  • The applications could not be rejected merely because earlier Aadhaar numbers had been suspended or cancelled due to biometric mismatch.
  • The Court directed UIDAI to take a final decision within four weeks from submission.
  • If rejection became necessary, UIDAI was required to communicate a reasoned written order.

Wider Institutional Directions

Beyond the individual dispute, the Court acknowledged the increasing number of Aadhaar-related grievances reaching constitutional courts across the country.

The Bench issued broader institutional directions requiring UIDAI authorities to:

  • Provide written communication regarding Aadhaar status and remedial procedures.
  • Maintain facilitation mechanisms for biometric mismatch and technical irregularity cases.
  • Avoid compelling citizens to repeatedly visit multiple offices unnecessarily.
  • Ensure humane and citizen-centric handling of cases involving students, senior citizens, and economically weaker sections.
  • Process biometric rectification and re-enrollment requests expeditiously.

Legal Principles Emerging from the Judgment

  • Digital identity systems must remain constitutionally fair and accessible.
  • Administrative authorities cannot indefinitely delay statutory remedies.
  • Technological irregularities cannot permanently deprive citizens of identity credentials.
  • Biometric safeguards must coexist with procedural fairness.
  • State authorities are obligated to adopt citizen-centric grievance mechanisms.

Conclusion

The judgment in Rohit & Rahul Nikalje v. UIDAI marks an important step in balancing technological governance with constitutional protections. The Bombay High Court recognized that while Aadhaar serves a legitimate public purpose, its implementation cannot ignore human realities and administrative fairness.

The ruling reinforces a fundamental constitutional principle — technology must serve citizens, not exclude them.

As India continues expanding digital governance infrastructure, this decision is likely to become an important precedent in ensuring that identity systems remain lawful, transparent, and humane.
